1 The Governing Physics

Fluid dynamics tracks the continuum mechanics of moving liquids and gases. Analyzing the motion of every individual molecule is computationally impossible, so we adopt a macroscopic approach. We treat the fluid as a continuous medium and analyze the flow field.

To derive the fundamental equations cleanly, we define an Ideal Fluid. An ideal fluid simplifies the Navier-Stokes equations by assuming four strict conditions:

Incompressible: Density ($\rho$) is uniform and constant. (Valid for most liquids and gases at low speeds).
Non-viscous: There is no internal friction or drag forces between adjacent fluid layers.
Irrotational: The fluid has no net angular momentum about any point; small objects won't spin as they move.
Steady-flow: The velocity at any given point in space remains constant over time.

Under these conditions, fluid behavior is strictly bounded by two fundamental conservation laws of the universe:

  • Conservation of Mass: What goes in must come out. The total mass entering a pipe section must exactly equal the mass exiting it.
  • Conservation of Energy: The total mechanical work done on a fluid element by external pressure differences must equal its net change in kinetic and potential energy.

2 The Equation of Continuity

Consider a fluid flowing through a conduit of varying cross-sectional area. Because matter cannot be created or destroyed, the mass flow rate ($\frac{dm}{dt}$) must be constant.

The mass of fluid moving past a point in time $\Delta t$ is $\Delta m = \rho \Delta V$, where $\Delta V$ is volume. Volume can be written as the cross-sectional area $A$ multiplied by the distance traveled $\Delta x = v \Delta t$. Therefore:

$$\frac{\Delta m}{\Delta t} = \rho \cdot A \cdot v = \text{Constant}$$

For an incompressible liquid, the density $\rho$ is constant and can be divided out. This leaves us with the Volume Flow Rate ($Q = A \cdot v$), which must remain strictly constant across all cross-sections. This yields the Equation of Continuity:

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

Volume Flow Rate ($Q = A \cdot v$)

Physical Intuition

If a pipe narrows (Area $A$ decreases), the fluid must speed up (Velocity $v$ increases) to ensure the same volume of fluid gets through per second. This is why placing your thumb over the end of a garden hose makes the water spray out much faster!

3 Bernoulli's Equation & Energy

While the Continuity Equation stems from the conservation of mass, Bernoulli's Equation is a direct consequence of the Work-Energy Theorem applied to fluids.

Work-Energy on a Fluid Streamline

Diagram showing a fluid element of volume $\Delta V$ moving from state 1 to state 2.

The Mathematical Derivation

To derive Bernoulli's Equation, we evaluate the net work done on a fluid element of mass $\Delta m$ and volume $\Delta V$ as it moves along a streamline. The fluid behind it pushes it forward with pressure $P_1$, doing positive work. The fluid ahead of it pushes back with pressure $P_2$, doing negative work.

$$W_{\text{net}} = F_1 \Delta x_1 - F_2 \Delta x_2$$ $$W_{\text{net}} = (P_1 A_1) \Delta x_1 - (P_2 A_2) \Delta x_2$$

Since Volume $V = A \Delta x$, and the volume pushed at point 1 must equal the volume received at point 2 ($\Delta V$), the net work done by fluid pressure is:

$$W_{\text{net}} = (P_1 - P_2)\Delta V$$

By the Work-Energy Theorem, this net work must equal the change in the fluid element's kinetic ($\Delta K$) and gravitational potential ($\Delta U$) energies:

$$\Delta K = \frac{1}{2}\Delta m (v_2^2 - v_1^2), \quad \Delta U = \Delta m g (y_2 - y_1)$$ $$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta K + \Delta U$$ $$(P_1 - P_2)\Delta V = \frac{1}{2}\Delta m (v_2^2 - v_1^2) + \Delta m g (y_2 - y_1)$$

Finally, we divide the entire equation by the volume $\Delta V$. Recognizing that mass over volume is density ($\rho = \frac{\Delta m}{\Delta V}$), and rearranging so all point-1 variables are on the left and point-2 on the right, we arrive at the crowning achievement of classical fluid dynamics:

$$P_1 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 + \rho g y_1 = P_2 + \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g y_2 = \text{Constant}$$

Understanding the Terms

  • $P$ (Static Pressure): The thermodynamic pressure of the fluid.
  • $\frac{1}{2}\rho v^2$ (Dynamic Pressure): Kinetic energy per unit volume.
  • $\rho g y$ (Hydrostatic Pressure): Potential energy per unit volume.

4 Application: Torricelli's Law

One of the most elegant applications of Bernoulli's equation is finding the speed of fluid exiting a spigot at the bottom of an open tank. This is known as Torricelli's Law.

Let Point 1 be the open top of the tank, and Point 2 be the exiting stream.

  • Both points are exposed to the atmosphere, so $P_1 = P_2 = P_{\text{atm}}$. They cancel out.
  • The tank is very large compared to the hole, so the water level drops extremely slowly. We can approximate $v_1 \approx 0$.
Bernoulli's equation simplifies from the massive full version to just:

$$\rho g y_1 = \frac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2 + \rho g y_2$$

Solving for the exit velocity $v_2$, where $h = y_1 - y_2$ (the depth of the hole below the surface):

$$v = \sqrt{2gh}$$

Remarkably, this is the exact same speed an object would attain if it were dropped in free fall from a height $h$. Let's visualize this with a simulation of water trajectory.

5 Problem-Solving Master Strategy

  1. 1

    Establish Two Reference Points

    Always choose point 1 and point 2 along a single continuous streamline. Pick locations where variables are either completely known, explicitly asked for, or safely approximateable (e.g., the large open top of a tank where $v \approx 0$).

  2. 2

    Set a Universal Baseline for Height

    Define where $y=0$ is. It is usually best to set the lowest point in your problem to $y=0$ so that no $y$-variables are negative. Consistency is key; $\rho g y$ is potential energy, and potential energy needs a strict reference frame.

  3. 3

    Apply Continuity First

    Before writing out the long Bernoulli equation, use the Equation of Continuity ($A_1v_1 = A_2v_2$) to express one velocity in terms of the other. This prevents you from having an unsolvable equation with two unknowns ($v_1$ and $v_2$).

  4. 4

    Watch Gauge vs. Absolute Pressure

    Ensure both $P_1$ and $P_2$ are using the same scale. If a fluid surface is open to the air, its absolute pressure is $1\text{ atm}$ ($1.013 \times 10^5\text{ Pa}$). If the problem gives you "gauge pressure," add $P_{\text{atm}}$ to get absolute pressure before plugging it into Bernoulli's.
