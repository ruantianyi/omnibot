1. The Core Concept

A Riemann sum is a foundational calculus approximation technique used to estimate the exact area under a continuous curve over an interval $[a, b]$.

By partitioning the domain into $n$ discrete rectangular strips, calculating their individual areas, and summing them up, the total value approaches the definitive integral as the number of strips approaches infinity. This forms the bridge between discrete approximation and continuous integration.

Mathematical Formulations

Let $f(x)$ be a continuous function bounded on $[a, b]$. We divide the interval into $n$ equal subintervals. The width of each subinterval, denoted as $\Delta x$, is defined as:

$$\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n}$$

The total area under the curve is approximated by summing the areas of these rectangles. Depending on where the rectangle heights sample the function $f(x)$, the sums are classified into different types:

Left Riemann Sum

Coordinates evaluated at the left boundary of each subinterval. Underestimates increasing functions.

$x_i^* = a + i\Delta x$ for $i=0$ to $n-1$

Right Riemann Sum

Coordinates evaluated at the right boundary of each subinterval. Overestimates increasing functions.

$x_i^* = a + i\Delta x$ for $i=1$ to $n$

The general approximation formula for the area is:

$$\text{Area} \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x$$

The Definite Integral Limit

Taking the mathematical limit as the number of rectangles $n$ approaches infinity (meaning $\Delta x \to 0$) yields the exact Definite Integral:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx$$

Problem-Solving Master Strategy

1

Find Subinterval Width ($\Delta x$)

Always compute $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$ first. This represents the fixed base width of every rectangle in your approximation.

2

Generate the Coordinate Sequence ($x_i$)

Write out the list of evaluating points based on your sum type. Let's look at an example for a Right sum on interval $[1, 3]$ with $n=4$.

Δx = (3 - 1) / 4 = 0.5

x₀ = 1.0 (Start)
x₁ = 1.5 (Eval 1)
x₂ = 2.0 (Eval 2)
x₃ = 2.5 (Eval 3)
x₄ = 3.0 (Eval 4 - End)
3

Evaluate and Sum

Compute the function output $f(x_i)$ at each coordinate point, sum the values, and multiply the final accumulated height sum by the width $\Delta x$. Factoring out $\Delta x$ saves calculation time.

$$\text{Area} \approx \Delta x \big[ f(x_1) + f(x_2) + f(x_3) + f(x_4) \big]$$

Integration Boundaries Properties

The limits of integration $[a, b]$ define the domain over which area is accumulated. Modifying these boundaries follows strict mathematical properties essential for solving complex integrals.

Reversal of Limits

Integrating backwards reverses the sign of the accumulated area.

$$\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = -\int_{b}^{a} f(x) dx$$

Zero Length Interval

If the upper and lower limits are identical, no area is accumulated.

$$\int_{a}^{a} f(x) dx = 0$$

Additive Interval Property

Integrals can be split at any intermediate point $c$.

$$\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = \int_{a}^{c} f(x) dx + \int_{c}^{b} f(x) dx$$
