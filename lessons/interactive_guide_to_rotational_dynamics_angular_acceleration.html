Rotational Dynamics &
Angular Acceleration

Master the physics of rotating bodies. From the vector mechanics of torque to the intricate energy distributions of rolling objects, this guide maps Newton's laws into the angular domain.

1. The Governing Physics

Rotational dynamics maps Newton's classical laws of linear motion into an angular coordinate system. In linear kinematics, we analyze objects moving along straight paths. In rotational kinematics, we analyze extended, rigid bodies spinning around an axis.

A critical realization is that every point mass within a rigid rotating body experiences a linear acceleration that depends heavily on its distance ($r$) from the axis of rotation. While the entire object shares the same angular velocity, the linear velocity varies.

The Angular-Linear Dictionary

To master rotational dynamics, you must learn to translate between linear and angular variables.

Position: $x \rightarrow \theta$ (Angle)
Velocity: $v \rightarrow \omega$
Acceleration: $a \rightarrow \alpha$
Inertia: $m \rightarrow I$
Force: $F \rightarrow \tau$ (Torque)
Momentum: $p \rightarrow L$

2. Mathematical Derivations: Torque ($\vec{\tau}$)

To alter a system's angular velocity ($\vec{\omega}$), a net external torque ($\vec{\tau}$) must be applied. Torque is the rotational equivalent of force. It measures how effectively a force can cause an object to rotate.

The Vector Cross Product Definition

The torque generated by a force $\vec{F}$ applied at a position vector $\vec{r}$ relative to a pivot point is defined via the vector cross product:

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \implies \tau = r F \sin\phi$$

Where $\phi$ is the angle between the position vector $\vec{r}$ and the force vector $\vec{F}$.

3. Moment of Inertia & Mass Distribution

A net torque acts against the system's rotational inertia—defined formally as the moment of inertia ($I$). Unlike linear mass ($m$), which is an intrinsic property, the moment of inertia depends heavily on how that mass is distributed relative to the axis of rotation.

Discrete Point Masses

For a collection of distinct point masses, sum the products of each mass and the square of its perpendicular distance from the axis:

$$I = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i r_i^2$$

Continuous Solid Bodies

For continuous solid masses, the summation generalizes to a volume integral involving mass density ($\rho$):

$$I = \int r^2 \, dm = \int \rho r^2 \, dV$$

The Parallel-Axis Theorem

Formulas for $I$ are typically given for an axis passing through the body's Center of Mass ($I_{\text{cm}}$). If the actual rotation axis is shifted parallel by a distance $d$ away from the center of mass, calculate the new moment of inertia using:

$$I_{\text{new}} = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2$$

This theorem highlights that it is always "hardest" to rotate an object around an axis that is far from its center of mass.

4. Newton's 2nd Law & Rolling Energy

Newton's Second Law for rotation unites the concepts of Torque and Inertia to yield angular acceleration:

$$\vec{\tau}_{\text{net}} = I\vec{\alpha} \quad \text{where} \quad \vec{\alpha} = \frac{d\vec{\omega}}{dt}$$

Rolling Without Slipping

When a solid body rolls without slipping along a surface, static friction does zero work (the contact point is instantaneously at rest). Its total kinetic energy splits perfectly into translational and rotational components:

$$K_{\text{total}} = K_{\text{trans}} + K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2$$

Because it rolls without slipping, the crucial kinematic constraint couples these two terms, allowing you to solve for final velocities seamlessly:

$$v_{\text{cm}} = \omega R \quad \text{and} \quad a_{\text{cm}} = \alpha R$$

5. Problem-Solving Master Strategy

1

Draw Extended Free-Body Diagrams

Standard point-mass FBDs are insufficient for rotational dynamics. You must place force vectors precisely at their physical points of application. For example, gravity always acts at the center of mass, while tension acts at the outer rim of a pulley, and friction acts exactly at the surface contact point.

2

Apply the Parallel-Axis Theorem (If Needed)

Determine the axis of rotation. If the object rotates around a hinge or pivot point that is not its center of mass, immediately calculate the new moment of inertia using $I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2$ before proceeding with dynamics equations.

3

Set Up Coupled Equations

Complex systems (like a mass falling while unwinding a pulley) require setting up independent systems of equations and coupling them:

  • Write $\sum F = ma$ for purely linear objects.
  • Write $\sum \tau = I\alpha$ for rotating components.
  • Link the systems strictly using the constraint equation: $a = \alpha R$.
4

Check for Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero ($\sum \tau_{\text{ext}} = 0$), then the total angular momentum of the system is conserved ($L_i = L_f$). This is often true in collisions between spinning objects or when an object changes its own moment of inertia internally.

Knowledge Check

1. If you double the distance from the pivot point where a perpendicular force is applied, how does the resulting torque change?

2. A solid cylinder ($I=\frac{1}{2}MR^2$) and a hollow cylinder ($I=MR^2$) of equal mass and radius are released from rest at the top of an incline. Which reaches the bottom first?

3. An ice skater is spinning with their arms outstretched. When they pull their arms in tight to their body, what happens to their angular velocity?
