Unit 9: Oscillations & Waves

Simple Harmonic Motion & Resonance

Explore the universal physics of restoring forces. From the invisible vibrations of diatomic molecules to the catastrophic collapse of bridges, understanding oscillatory motion is fundamental to classical mechanics.

01

The Governing Physics

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) occurs when an object is systematically perturbed from a stable equilibrium position by a restoring force that scales linearly with displacement. Because this restoring force always points directly back toward the central origin, the accelerating path describes a continuous sine or cosine waveform.

Mathematical Derivations & Governing Calculus

Consider a mass $m$ linked to an ideal, massless spring with stiffness constant $k$. Applying Hooke's Law ($\vec{F} = -k\vec{x}$) to Newton's Second Law ($\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$) provides a linear second-order differential equation:

$$-k x = m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \implies \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \left(\frac{k}{m}\right)x = 0$$

By identifying the angular frequency squared as $\omega^2 = \frac{k}{m}$, this matches the characteristic SHM template $\ddot{x} + \omega^2 x = 0$. The general analytical solution for position over time is a trigonometric function:

$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$$

Taking successive derivatives with respect to time ($t$) provides the velocity ($v$) and acceleration ($a$) functions. Note the crucial phase shifts introduced by the derivatives:

Velocity Function

$$v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = -\omega A\sin(\omega t + \phi)$$
Max Velocity: $v_{\text{max}} = \omega A$ (Occurs at equilibrium, $x=0$)

Acceleration Function

$$a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = -\omega^2 A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$$
Max Acceleration: $a_{\text{max}} = \omega^2 A$ (Occurs at extremes, $x=\pm A$)

The absolute period $T$ of oscillation (the time it takes to complete one full cycle) is derived directly from the angular frequency $\omega$:

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$

Energy Conservation

In an ideal SHM system (with no non-conservative forces like friction or air resistance), the total mechanical energy $E_{\text{total}}$ remains strictly constant. It continuously transforms between Elastic Potential Energy ($U_s$) and Kinetic Energy ($K$).

  • Potential Energy: $U_s = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$
  • Kinetic Energy: $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$

Adding these together at any point in time yields the constant total energy, which is equal to the maximum potential energy (at $x=\pm A$) or the maximum kinetic energy (at $x=0$):

$$E_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv_{\text{max}}^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

Exam Trick: Use this energy conservation equation to easily bypass time parameters ($t$) when finding intermediate velocities at specific positions!

Forced Oscillations & Resonance

If an external periodic driving force ($F_{\text{drive}} = F_0 \cos(\omega_d t)$) is applied to the system, the resulting amplitude depends heavily on how close the driving frequency ($\omega_d$) is to the system's natural frequency ($\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$).

Resonance occurs when $\omega_d \approx \omega_0$. The energy transfer efficiency maximizes, causing the system's amplitude to grow dramatically. Without sufficient damping (friction), this can lead to catastrophic mechanical failure.

Lower damping leads to a sharper, higher resonance peak.

Steady-state amplitude equation: $$A(\omega_d) = \frac{F_0}{\sqrt{m^2(\omega_0^2 - \omega_d^2)^2 + b^2\omega_d^2}}$$
05

Problem-Solving Master Strategy

1. Verify the SHM Profile

Ensure the acceleration equation can be mathematically manipulated into the exact form $a = -\text{(constant)}x$. The square root of that strictly positive constant is always your angular frequency $\omega$.

2. Apply Energy Conservation

Use $E_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ whenever you need to relate position directly to velocity without knowing the elapsed time.

3. Phasing Calibration

Evaluate the phase constant $\phi$ using initial boundary conditions at $t=0$. If released from rest at maximum displacement, $\phi = 0$. If starting at equilibrium moving positively, $\phi = -\pi/2$.
