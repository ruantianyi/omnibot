Electrostatic Fields & Potential Mapping

A rigorous exploration of stationary electric charges, the vector fields they generate, and the scalar potential contours that map the energy geometry of space.

1 The Governing Physics

Electrostatic systems track forces exerted by stationary electric charges. The fundamental postulate of electrostatics is that every electric charge modifies the geometry of the space around it. This modification is not merely a mathematical abstraction, but a physically measurable reality that determines how other charged particles will behave when entering that space.

To understand this interaction without relying on "spooky action at a distance," physicists utilize two complementary models to map space:

The Electric Field ($\vec{E}$)

A vector field tracking force per unit charge. It answers the kinematic question: "If I place a test charge here, in what direction and with what magnitude will it be pushed?" It assigns a vector to every point $(x,y,z)$ in space.

Electric Potential ($V$)

A scalar field tracking electrical potential energy per unit charge. It answers the energetic question: "How much work is required to move a charge to this location?" It assigns a single number (magnitude, no direction) to every point in space.

2 Mathematical Derivations & Field Equations

Coulomb's Law

The fundamental force $\vec{F}$ between two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by a distance $r$ is an inverse-square law, governed by Coulomb's constant ($k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$):

$$ \vec{F} = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}\hat{r} $$

Vector Superposition of Fields

The electric field vector $\vec{E}$ generated by a configuration of source charges is found by the linear vector superposition of individual contributions. At a point $P$, the total field is the vector sum:

$$ \vec{E} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\frac{q_i}{r_i^2}\hat{r}_i $$

Note: Because $\vec{E}$ is a vector, you must break contributions into orthogonal $x, y, z$ components before summing. Magnitudes cannot simply be added algebraically.

Scalar Potential Integral & The Spatial Gradient

Because the electrostatic force is entirely conservative (curl $\vec{\nabla} \times \vec{E} = 0$), the work done moving a charge between two points is independent of the path taken. We define the scalar potential difference $\Delta V$ as the negative line integral of the electric field:

$$ \Delta V = V_2 - V_1 = -\int_{1}^{2} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} $$

Conversely, if the scalar potential field $V(x,y,z)$ is known, the vector electric field is precisely the spatial gradient of the potential, pointing in the direction of steepest descent (from high to low potential):

$$ \vec{E} = -\vec{\nabla}V = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\hat{i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\hat{j} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\hat{k}\right) $$

3 Field Lines & Equipotential Contours

Visualizing space via diagrams is critical to solving electrostatic problems. The dual concepts of field lines and equipotential lines map perfectly to the dual mathematical constructs of $\vec{E}$ and $V$.

  • Electric Field Lines: Directed trajectories tracking the path a positive, massless test charge would take. They emerge from positive source charges (sources) and terminate on negative ones (sinks). Density of lines indicates field magnitude.
  • Equipotential Lines: Spatial paths (or 3D surfaces) along which the electric potential $V$ remains perfectly constant. Moving a charge along these lines requires exactly zero work ($W=0$).

The Orthogonality Constraint Proof

Field lines and equipotential lines must intersect at exactly $90^\circ$ angles everywhere in space. Why?

Recall the relation $dV = -\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s}$. If you move a distance $d\vec{s}$ along an equipotential line, the change in potential is zero by definition ($dV = 0$). Therefore, the dot product $-\vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0$. Since neither the electric field magnitude nor the displacement magnitude is zero, the angle $\theta$ between them must be such that $\cos(\theta) = 0$, meaning $\theta = 90^\circ$.

4 Continuous Charge Distributions & Gauss's Law

When charges are not discrete points but are smeared continuously over lines, surfaces, or volumes, summation $\Sigma$ becomes integration $\int$. We define charge densities: linear ($\lambda = dq/dl$), surface ($\sigma = dq/dA$), and volume ($\rho = dq/dV$).

For highly symmetric geometries (infinite lines, infinite sheets, spheres), direct integration is replaced by Gauss's Law. Gauss's Law states that the net electric flux ($\Phi_E$) passing through any closed imaginary mathematical boundary (a Gaussian surface) is strictly proportional to the total enclosed charge:

$$ \Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enclosed}}}{\varepsilon_0} $$

The Symmetry Exploit: If you can choose a Gaussian surface where $\vec{E}$ is constant in magnitude and parallel to $d\vec{A}$ everywhere on the boundary, the integral collapses to $E \cdot A = Q_{\text{in}} / \varepsilon_0$.

5 Problem-Solving Master Strategy

For Electric Fields ($\vec{E}$)

  • Vector Superposition: When calculating $\vec{E}$, you must split your analysis into independent $x$ and $y$ vector components using $\cos\phi$ and $\sin\phi$ trigonometric modifiers.
  • Warning: Never add magnitudes directly (e.g., $E_{tot} \neq E_1 + E_2$). Magnitudes must be combined via the Pythagorean theorem.
  • Symmetry Identification: Always look for mirror symmetry before calculating. If a positive charge on the left cancels the x-component of a positive charge on the right, you can completely ignore integrating $dE_x$.

For Electric Potential ($V$)

  • Scalar Superposition: When calculating $V$, add values algebraically ($V = \sum \frac{kq_i}{r_i}$). No trig components required! It is a simple arithmetic sum.
  • Warning: You must include the algebraic positive or negative signs of the source charges directly in your sum. A negative charge subtracts from the total potential.
  • Integration Check: If integrating $V = \int \frac{k\,dq}{r}$, note that $r$ is often constant for a ring or arc. If $r$ is constant, you can pull it out, leaving $V = \frac{k}{r}\int dq = \frac{kQ_{tot}}{r}$.
