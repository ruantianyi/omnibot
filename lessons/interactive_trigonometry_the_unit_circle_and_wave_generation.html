1. The Unit Circle & Angle Measurement

The unit circle is defined as a circle of radius $r = 1$ centered at the origin $(0,0)$ in the Cartesian coordinate plane. Angles are measured standardly counterclockwise from the positive x-axis.

While angles in introductory geometry are measured in degrees, physical and analytical wave calculations overwhelmingly use radians. A full revolution around the unit circle corresponds to a circumference of $2\pi$, meaning $360^\circ = 2\pi ext{ rad}$.

2. Sine, Cosine & Tangent Functions

For any point $P(x,y)$ on the unit circle corresponding to angle $ heta$, the coordinates are directly defined by the primary trigonometric functions: $x = \cos( heta)$ and $y = \sin( heta)$.

The tangent function represents the slope of the line segment connecting the origin to point $P$, given by $ an( heta) = rac{\sin( heta)}{\cos( heta)} = rac{y}{x}$.

3. Wave Generation & Harmonic Motion

When a point moves along the unit circle at a constant angular frequency $\omega$, tracking its vertical displacement over time traces out a pure sinusoidal wave: $y(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \phi)$.

Here, $A$ represents amplitude, $\omega = 2\pi f$ is angular frequency, and $\phi$ is the initial phase angle. This mathematical equivalence forms the basis for Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) in physical systems.
