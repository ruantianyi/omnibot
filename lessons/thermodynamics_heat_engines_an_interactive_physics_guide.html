Thermodynamics & Ideal Gas Heat Engines

A rigorous exploration of macroscopic state parameters, cycle equations, and the theoretical limits of thermodynamic efficiency. Master the $P-V$ diagram and the Carnot Engine.

The Governing Physics

Thermodynamics tracks macroscopic state parameters—Pressure ($P$), Volume ($V$), Temperature ($T$), and Entropy ($S$)—as thermal systems interact.

A heat engine operates on a closed cyclic thermodynamic path. Its primary function is absorbing thermal energy ($Q_H$) from a high-temperature reservoir, converting a fraction of it into mechanical work ($W$), and exhausting the leftover low-grade thermal waste ($Q_C$) into a cold sink. Because the engine operates in a cycle, the working fluid (usually an ideal gas) constantly returns to its initial state.

Mathematical Derivations & Cycle Equations

The First Law of Thermodynamics

The fundamental statement of the Conservation of Energy for a thermal system.

$$\Delta U = Q - W$$
  • $\Delta U$: Change in internal energy (depends only on temperature for an ideal gas).
  • $Q$: Heat added to the system.
  • $W$: Work performed by the gas ($+W$ indicates expansion).

For a continuous path on a $P-V$ diagram, work is not a mere multiplication; it is evaluated via the geometric area under the curve using integral calculus:

$$W = \int_{V_1}^{V_2} P \, dV$$

The Four Fundamental Processes

Evaluating the work integral and applying the First Law yields simplified relations for four special thermodynamic paths:

Isobaric

Constant Pressure ($\Delta P = 0$)

The gas expands or compresses at a steady pressure.

$$W = P(V_2 - V_1)$$

Isochoric

Constant Volume ($\Delta V = 0$)

No spatial displacement occurs, thus no mechanical work is done.

$$W = 0 \implies \Delta U = Q$$

Isothermal

Constant Temperature ($\Delta T = 0$)

Internal energy is strictly tied to temp, so $\Delta U = 0$. Using $P = \frac{nRT}{V}$, the integral becomes a natural log.

$$Q = W = nRT\ln\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$

Adiabatic

No Heat Transfer ($Q = 0$)

The system is perfectly insulated. Expansion cools the gas natively. Follows $P V^\gamma = \text{Constant}$.

$$\Delta U = -W$$

Engine Efficiency & The Carnot Limit

The efficiency $\eta$ of any engine cycle is defined by the ratio of the desired output (net work) to the costly input (heat from the high-temperature reservoir):

$$\eta = \frac{W_{\text{net}}}{Q_H} = \frac{Q_H - |Q_C|}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{|Q_C|}{Q_H}$$

The Carnot Limit: Sadi Carnot proved that no heat engine operating between two temperatures can be more efficient than a reversible Carnot engine operating between those same two reservoirs. The upper theoretical limit depends solely on absolute temperatures (in Kelvin):

$$\eta_{\text{max}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}$$

Problem-Solving Master Strategy

1

Construct a State Table

Solve for $P$, $V$, and $T$ at every vertex (A, B, C, D) using the Ideal Gas Law ($PV = nRT$) before attempting to track energy metrics. The table grounds your variables.

2

Calculate Work Graphically

Sum up work values across all individual steps ($W_{\text{net}} = W_{AB} + W_{BC} + \dots$). Remember that a clockwise loop on a $P-V$ diagram signifies net positive work output (an engine). A counter-clockwise loop implies work is done on the system (a refrigerator/heat pump).

3

Enforce Kelvin Temperature Scale

This is the most common pitfall! Always convert Celsius to Kelvin ($T_K = T_C + 273.15$) before evaluating any thermodynamic equations, especially the Carnot Efficiency equation. Using Celsius will mathematically fail.
