Chapter 1: Geometric Foundations

Historical & Physical Context

The formal definition of planetary motion underwent a turbulent paradigm shift over centuries of scientific inquiry. Early astronomers, bound by the philosophical dogmas of absolute perfection, insisted that all celestial bodies must travel along perfectly circular paths around a stationary Earth. This geocentric, uniform-circular model mandated increasingly complex adjustments—such as epicycles upon epicycles—to account for the observable retrograde motions of the planets. It was not until the meticulously recorded astronomical data of Tycho Brahe was rigorously analyzed by Johannes Kepler that the true geometric nature of planetary tracks was unveiled. Kepler realized that a circle was merely a highly specific sub-case of a broader family of curves known as conic sections. By recognizing that planetary paths were elliptical, with the Sun situated strictly at one focal point rather than the geometric center, Kepler unified the heavens under a single, elegant mathematical framework. This monumental realization eradicated the need for arbitrary epicycles and laid the indispensable geometric foundation upon which Isaac Newton would later build the law of universal gravitation. Understanding the exact geometry of the ellipse is therefore not just an exercise in pure mathematics, but the critical first step in unraveling the true physical kinematics of the cosmos.

Historically, Apollonius of Perga extensively documented the properties of conics in antiquity, yet these abstract curves were largely considered theoretical novelties devoid of practical earthly application. The integration of Apollonian geometry with physical astronomy represented a masterclass in interdisciplinary synthesis. The realization that planetary distances inherently dilate and contract as they progress along their elliptical track profoundly altered our understanding of energy distribution. The concepts of periapsis (the point of closest approach) and apoapsis (the point of maximum recession) emerged as the definitive geometric boundaries enclosing the dynamic energy states of a satellite. This dynamic oscillation in orbital radius fundamentally shatters the illusion of constant-velocity transit, introducing a profoundly kinetic, perpetually accelerating reality.

Rigorous Mathematical Derivation

To comprehend orbital kinematics, we must systematically derive the mathematical architecture of the ellipse from basic Euclidean axioms. By formal definition, an ellipse is the locus of all points in a two-dimensional Cartesian plane such that the sum of the distances from any point on the curve to two fixed points (the foci) remains strictly constant. Let these foci be denoted as $F_1$ and $F_2$. For any arbitrary point $P(x,y)$ residing on the perimeter of the ellipse, the defining geometric constraint is universally expressed as:

$$ d(F_1, P) + d(F_2, P) = 2a $$

Here, the scalar parameter $a$ represents the length of the semi-major axis—the longest radius of the ellipse. If we establish a Cartesian coordinate system with the geometric origin located perfectly between the two foci, the coordinates of the foci are given by $(-c, 0)$ and $(c, 0)$, where $c$ defines the linear eccentricity. Applying the standard Euclidean distance formula, we construct the fundamental algebraic relationship:

$$ \sqrt{(x+c)^2 + y^2} + \sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} = 2a $$

To isolate the variables and eliminate the radical expressions, we isolate one square root and mathematically square both sides of the equation. This arduous algebraic expansion eventually simplifies into the canonical Cartesian form of the ellipse. First, isolating the left radical:

$$ \sqrt{(x+c)^2 + y^2} = 2a - \sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} $$

Squaring both sides and expanding the binomials yields:

$$ (x+c)^2 + y^2 = 4a^2 - 4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} + (x-c)^2 + y^2 $$

Expanding the internal quadratics $(x^2 + 2xc + c^2)$ and canceling common terms ($x^2, c^2, y^2$) across the equality boundary drastically reduces the expression to:

$$ 4xc = 4a^2 - 4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} $$

Dividing out the common factor of $4$ and isolating the remaining radical once more:

$$ a\sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} = a^2 - xc $$

Squaring a second time eliminates the final radical, allowing us to group the $x$ and $y$ terms:

$$ a^2(x^2 - 2xc + c^2 + y^2) = a^4 - 2a^2xc + x^2c^2 $$

Factoring this expression reveals the deep underlying relationship between the geometric constants $a$ and $c$. By defining a new constant $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$ (which physically represents the semi-minor axis), we arrive at the universally recognized, dimensionless Cartesian equation for an unrotated ellipse:

$$ \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1 $$

However, in astrodynamics, utilizing polar coordinates anchored exactly at the primary focus (the Sun) is far more analytically powerful than a Cartesian system centered on empty space. The transformation into polar form utilizes the radius $r$ and the true anomaly angle $\theta$. Through trigonometric substitution, the orbital equation is reformulated as $r(\theta) = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1 + e \cos\theta}$, where $e = c/a$ is the numerical eccentricity. This pivotal equation dictates the precise radial distance of a satellite at any given angular displacement.

Physical Kinematics & Behaviors

The polar orbital equation mathematically enforces the dynamic, breathing nature of an elliptical orbit. Because the denominator contains a cosine function bounded strictly between $+1$ and $-1$, the radial distance $r$ is continuously forced to oscillate between absolute minimum and absolute maximum limits. When the satellite crosses the periapsis line (where $\theta = 0^\circ$), the cosine function maximizes to $+1$. The denominator becomes $1 + e$, driving the overall fraction to its smallest possible value. This confirms the physical reality: periapsis is the point of closest approach, mathematically defined as $r_p = a(1-e)$.

Conversely, exactly half an orbit later, the satellite crosses the apoapsis boundary ($\theta = 180^\circ$). Here, the cosine function collapses to $-1$, dropping the denominator to $1 - e$. This inflates the fraction to its maximum geometric limit, calculating the apoapsis distance as $r_a = a(1+e)$. As the orbital eccentricity $e$ approaches zero, the bounds $r_p$ and $r_a$ mathematically converge onto a single unified radius $r = a$, recovering the perfectly circular orbit. Should $e$ reach or exceed $1.0$, the denominator approaches zero, blasting the radial distance out to infinity—signifying a catastrophic ejection from the system via parabolic or hyperbolic escape trajectories.

Chapter 2: Keplerian Dynamics & Area Sweeps

Historical & Physical Context

Having established the geometric shape of the planetary orbit as a pristine ellipse, Johannes Kepler faced an even more daunting physical paradox: the velocity of the planet was non-uniform. Prior to the seventeenth century, the concept of constant velocity was dogmatically linked to celestial perfection. If a planet traveled in an ellipse, its varying speed along the perimeter defied all established classical physics. Kepler analyzed decades of tracking data compiled by his mentor, Tycho Brahe, specifically focusing on the orbit of Mars. Through sheer brute-force numerical integration—performed entirely by hand without the aid of modern calculus or computational devices—Kepler noticed an exquisite mathematical symmetry hiding within the apparent chaos of the planet's accelerating and decelerating track. He realized that while the linear velocity vector $\vec{v}$ constantly fluctuates in magnitude, the rate at which the imaginary line connecting the Sun to the planet sweeps out two-dimensional geometric area remains perfectly invariant. This profound discovery, published in 1609 as Kepler’s Second Law (the Law of Equal Areas), shattered the illusion of constant speed. It proved that the universe conserves something far deeper than linear velocity: it conserves angular momentum. The physics governing this conservation dictates that as a celestial body falls into the gravitational well towards periapsis, its radial distance $r$ shrinks, forcing its angular velocity $\omega$ to violently spike upwards to maintain the constant area sweep rate. This mechanism acts as the cosmic engine driving all orbital mechanics.

Furthermore, Kepler spent another decade searching for a unifying harmonic relationship that linked the orbits of completely different planets across the solar system. In 1619, he published his Third Law (the Harmonic Law), establishing that the square of a planet's orbital period $T$ is directly proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis $a$. This was the first true unified field observation in astrophysics, proving that the Sun exerts a centralized, distance-dependent force that governs all satellites uniformly, regardless of their mass or specific geometric eccentricity. It was this exact proportionality that later allowed Sir Isaac Newton to derive the inverse-square law of universal gravitation.

Rigorous Mathematical Derivation

To rigorously prove Kepler's Second Law, we must deploy infinitesimal calculus to analyze the area swept by the position vector $\vec{r}$. Consider a planet moving along an arbitrary curve over an infinitesimally small time interval $dt$. The position vector traverses a minute angular displacement $d\theta$. The geometric area swept out during this infinitesimal slice of time can be modeled as a vanishingly thin triangle. The base of this triangle is the radial distance $r$, and the height (the arc length) is approximately $r d\theta$. The differential area $dA$ is calculated using the standard triangle area formula:

$$ dA = \frac{1}{2} (\text{base}) \times (\text{height}) = \frac{1}{2} r (r d\theta) = \frac{1}{2} r^2 d\theta $$

To determine the rate at which this area is swept out over time (the areal velocity), we divide both sides of the differential equation by the time interval $dt$:

$$ \frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2} r^2 \frac{d\theta}{dt} $$

The term $\frac{d\theta}{dt}$ is the instantaneous angular velocity of the planet, conventionally denoted by the Greek letter $\omega$. Thus, the areal velocity is $\frac{1}{2} r^2 \omega$. To connect this geometric rate to physical kinematics, we must invoke the definition of angular momentum $L$. For a point mass $m$ rotating about a central axis, the magnitude of its angular momentum is defined by the cross product of the position vector and the linear momentum vector: $L = |\vec{r} \times \vec{p}| = m r v_{\perp}$. Since the perpendicular component of velocity is precisely $v_{\perp} = r \omega$, we can substitute this into the angular momentum equation:

$$ L = m r (r \omega) = m r^2 \omega \implies r^2 \omega = \frac{L}{m} $$

By substituting the term $r^2 \omega$ back into our areal velocity equation, we obtain the ultimate derivation of Kepler's Second Law: $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{L}{2m}$. Because the gravitational force is purely central (meaning it exerts strictly zero torque on the system), the angular momentum $L$ is a rigidly conserved constant. Since the mass $m$ is also constant, the entire ratio $\frac{L}{2m}$ is invariant. Therefore, the areal velocity $\frac{dA}{dt}$ is perfectly constant throughout the entirety of the orbit, irrefutably proving that equal areas are swept in equal times.

Physical Kinematics & Behaviors

The conservation of areal velocity dictates severe kinematic consequences for highly eccentric orbits. In order to maintain the constant mathematical product of $\frac{1}{2}r^2\omega$, a planet falling toward periapsis (where $r$ drastically minimizes) must compensate by experiencing an explosive exponential increase in its angular velocity $\omega$. This results in a "whip-cracking" effect where comets and highly elliptical satellites spend the vast majority of their orbital period slowly crawling through the outer edges of the solar system (apoapsis), only to violently accelerate and slingshot around the star in a matter of days or weeks. This behavior is heavily utilized in orbital mechanics to execute gravitational assist maneuvers, stealing microscopic fractions of a planet's angular momentum to eject probes into deep space.

Kepler's Third Law ($T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}a^3$) further defines the macro-kinematics of the solar system. As the semi-major axis $a$ increases, the orbital period $T$ increases disproportionately due to the cubic power. A planet twice as far from the Sun does not merely take twice as long to orbit; it takes $2^{1.5} \approx 2.82$ times as long. This kinematic reality enforces a stark velocity gradient across the solar system, where inner planets like Mercury whip around the Sun at blistering speeds, while distant ice giants like Neptune plod along at a fraction of the velocity, trapped in the weak outer gradients of the gravitational well.

Chapter 3: Vector Forces & Speeds

Historical & Physical Context

The transition from purely geometric planetary tracking to true dynamic physics required the genius of Sir Isaac Newton. Prior to the publication of the Principia Mathematica in 1687, the concept of a "force" acting instantaneously across the vast, empty vacuum of space was viewed with intense skepticism. The prevailing Cartesian philosophy argued that motion was transferred exclusively through direct physical contact via swirling ethereal vortices. Newton’s formulation of Universal Gravitation dismantled this framework by proving that a single, invisible, inverse-square force was solely responsible for both an apple falling to the Earth and the Moon remaining tethered in its orbit. This profound conceptual leap firmly established that the continuous, curved path of an elliptical orbit is not a state of zero-force equilibrium, but rather a state of perpetual, chaotic free-fall. In an elliptical orbit, the gravitational force vector $\vec{F}_g$ is rarely perfectly perpendicular to the satellite's velocity vector $\vec{v}$. Because work is physically defined as the dot product of force and displacement ($W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r}$), this geometric misalignment means that gravity actively performs mechanical work on the satellite throughout its transit, continuously injecting or extracting kinetic energy and driving the severe velocity fluctuations observed by Kepler.

Rigorous Mathematical Derivation

To quantify this continuous acceleration, we analyze the vector components of Newtonian gravity. The universal gravitational force acting between a central body of mass $M$ and a satellite of mass $m$ separated by a position vector $\vec{r}$ is given by:

$$ \vec{F}_g = - \frac{G M m}{r^2} \hat{r} $$

According to Newton's Second Law ($\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$), the instantaneous acceleration vector of the satellite is simply $\vec{a} = - \frac{\mu}{r^2} \hat{r}$, where $\mu = GM$ is the standard gravitational parameter. To determine how this acceleration affects the kinetic energy, we evaluate the mechanical work done by gravity over an infinitesimal path segment $d\vec{r}$:

$$ dW = \vec{F}_g \cdot d\vec{r} = \left( - \frac{\mu m}{r^2} \hat{r} \right) \cdot (d\vec{r}) $$

The differential displacement vector $d\vec{r}$ can be decomposed into radial and tangential components: $d\vec{r} = dr\,\hat{r} + r\,d\theta\,\hat{\theta}$. Because the dot product of orthogonal unit vectors ($\hat{r} \cdot \hat{\theta}$) is exactly zero, only the radial component survives the multiplication. Thus, the dot product cleanly collapses to:

$$ dW = - \frac{\mu m}{r^2} dr $$

Integrating this differential work equation from an initial radial distance $r_1$ to a final distance $r_2$ yields the total macroscopic work done by the gravitational field: $W = \int_{r_1}^{r_2} -\frac{\mu m}{r^2} dr = \mu m \left( \frac{1}{r_2} - \frac{1}{r_1} \right)$. By the Work-Energy Theorem ($W = \Delta K$), this exact quantity must equal the change in the satellite's kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_1^2$). This rigorous derivation proves unequivocally that radial displacement alone ($dr$) governs the kinetic energy fluctuations in an elliptical orbit. When a satellite falls inward ($dr < 0$), positive work is done, kinetic energy surges, and the velocity vector drastically increases in magnitude.

Physical Kinematics & Behaviors

The resulting kinematic behaviors are most clearly observable at the apsides. At exactly periapsis and exactly apoapsis, the radial displacement component $dr$ briefly drops to absolute zero. At these two critical coordinate boundaries, the velocity vector $\vec{v}$ is perfectly, $90^\circ$ perpendicular to the gravitational vector $\vec{F}_g$. The dot product hits zero, meaning gravity instantaneously performs zero mechanical work. This forces the derivative of kinetic energy to zero, defining mathematically why velocity reaches its absolute maximum peak at periapsis and its absolute minimum trough at apoapsis before smoothly reversing polarity.

Astrodynamic engineers exploit these specific kinematic peaks when executing Hohmann transfer orbits. By intentionally firing engines exactly at periapsis (where the spacecraft is already traveling at its absolute maximum kinetic speed), the propulsion system maximizes its energetic efficiency due to the Oberth effect. Conversely, firing engines at apoapsis requires vastly less raw thrust to fundamentally warp the geometric shape of the orbit because the rigid angular momentum vector is at its most vulnerable, minimal kinetic state.

Chapter 4: Angular Momentum & Conic Boundaries

Historical & Physical Context

The classical description of the solar system treats the planets as a perfectly flat, two-dimensional clockwork mechanism. If one were to observe the Solar System from deep interstellar space, it would appear as an impossibly thin disk of matter spinning around the central star. This absolute geometric flatness is not a random initial condition of the universe, but rather an inflexible physical consequence of the conservation of angular momentum. In classical mechanics, the angular momentum vector $\vec{L}$ of a body can only be altered by the application of an external torque ($\vec{\tau}$). Because Isaac Newton defined universal gravitation as a strictly central, radial force—meaning the gravitational pull is always directed straight down the center line of the position vector $\vec{r}$ connecting the two masses—there is absolutely zero lateral or orthogonal force generated by gravity. Without an orthogonal force component, it is physically impossible to generate torque. As a result, the angular momentum vector of any isolated orbiting body is eternally locked, both in magnitude and in its spatial, three-dimensional orientation.

The direction of this conserved vector defines a rigid axis pointing out into deep space (for Earth, this vector generally points toward the North Ecliptic Pole). Because the velocity and position vectors of the planet must constantly remain perfectly perpendicular to this rigid angular momentum axis, the entire orbital path is strictly confined to a singular mathematical plane. The satellite cannot drift "up" or "down" relative to this plane unless acted upon by a secondary, external gravitational source (such as the perturbing tug of a nearby gas giant) or a commanded thruster burn. This concept of conic planar rigidity is the foundational architecture underlying all modern satellite constellation designs, from GPS to low-earth orbit communication arrays.

Rigorous Mathematical Derivation

To mathematically prove the absolute planar rigidity of an orbit, we evaluate the cross product definition of angular momentum and its time derivative. For a satellite of mass $m$, the specific angular momentum $\vec{h}$ (angular momentum per unit mass) is defined as the cross product of the position vector $\vec{r}$ and the velocity vector $\vec{v}$:

$$ \vec{h} = \vec{r} \times \vec{v} $$

To determine how this vector evolves over time, we take the time derivative of the equation, applying the standard product rule for vector cross products:

$$ \frac{d\vec{h}}{dt} = \left( \frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} \times \vec{v} \right) + \left( \vec{r} \times \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} \right) $$

We can simplify this equation by identifying the physical derivatives. The derivative of position ($\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt}$) is simply the velocity vector $\vec{v}$. The derivative of velocity ($\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$) is the acceleration vector $\vec{a}$. Substituting these physical terms back into the product rule yields:

$$ \frac{d\vec{h}}{dt} = (\vec{v} \times \vec{v}) + (\vec{r} \times \vec{a}) $$

In vector algebra, the cross product of any vector with itself is identically zero, because there is no angle between them (the sine of $0^\circ$ is zero). Therefore, the first term $(\vec{v} \times \vec{v})$ instantly collapses to zero. For the second term, we recall from Newton’s laws that gravitational acceleration $\vec{a} = -\frac{\mu}{r^3} \vec{r}$. Because the acceleration vector is merely a scalar multiple of the position vector $\vec{r}$ pointing in the exact opposite direction, the two vectors are perfectly anti-parallel. The cross product of parallel or anti-parallel vectors is also identically zero.

$$ \frac{d\vec{h}}{dt} = 0 + (\vec{r} \times -k\vec{r}) = 0 $$

Because the time derivative of the specific angular momentum vector is precisely zero ($\frac{d\vec{h}}{dt} = \vec{0}$), the vector $\vec{h}$ must be a strict constant of motion. This proves that $\vec{h}$ never changes its scalar length, nor does it ever change its 3D spatial direction. Since $\vec{h}$ is defined as $\vec{r} \times \vec{v}$, both $\vec{r}$ and $\vec{v}$ must always lie in the exact same geometric plane perpendicular to $\vec{h}$. The orbit is immutably trapped within this two-dimensional conic section.

Chapter 5: Energetics & Vis-Viva

Historical & Physical Context

The culmination of orbital mechanics lies in the mastery of orbital energetics. While Kepler provided the geometric shape and Newton provided the dynamic force vectors, it was the application of the conservation of mechanical energy that finally allowed aerospace engineers to accurately predict velocities at any arbitrary point along an orbit, not just at the apsides. The total mechanical energy $\epsilon$ of an orbiting satellite is the sum of its positive kinetic energy (the energy of motion) and its negative gravitational potential energy (the energy debt required to climb out of the gravity well). Because gravity is a conservative force, this total specific energy $\epsilon$ remains rigidly constant. As a spacecraft plummets toward periapsis, it converts its stored potential energy into explosive kinetic velocity. As it climbs back toward apoapsis, it bleeds off that velocity to repay the potential energy debt. This delicate, perpetual balancing act is formally defined by the Vis-Viva equation (Latin for "living force"), arguably the single most important mathematical formula in applied astrodynamics.

The specific energy state $\epsilon$ intrinsically defines the macroscopic geometry of the orbit. If the total energy is precisely zero ($\epsilon = 0$), the satellite possesses exactly enough kinetic energy to reach infinite distance and coast to a halt; this forms a parabolic escape trajectory. If the energy is positive ($\epsilon > 0$), the satellite will escape the system entirely with excess hyperbolic velocity, never to return. But for all bound, repeating planetary orbits—including all circles and ellipses—the total mechanical energy is strictly negative ($\epsilon < 0$). This negative sign physically signifies that the object is permanently trapped within the gravitational well of the central star, lacking the necessary velocity to achieve escape.

Rigorous Mathematical Derivation

To derive the Vis-Viva equation, we begin with the definition of specific mechanical energy $\epsilon$ (energy per unit mass). It is the sum of specific kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}v^2$) and specific potential energy ($-\frac{\mu}{r}$):

$$ \epsilon = \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r} $$

Because $\epsilon$ is a conserved constant throughout the orbit, we can evaluate it at a known boundary condition to find its true value. Let us evaluate the energy strictly at periapsis ($r_p$). We know from geometry that $r_p = a(1-e)$. We also know from angular momentum conservation ($h = r_p v_p$) and the conic parameter equation ($h = \sqrt{\mu a (1-e^2)}$) that the velocity at periapsis can be expressed purely in terms of orbital parameters. Squaring the velocity yields:

$$ v_p^2 = \frac{h^2}{r_p^2} = \frac{\mu a (1-e^2)}{a^2(1-e)^2} = \frac{\mu (1-e)(1+e)}{a(1-e)^2} = \frac{\mu(1+e)}{a(1-e)} $$

We now substitute this $v_p^2$ back into the specific energy equation evaluated at periapsis:

$$ \epsilon = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{\mu(1+e)}{a(1-e)} \right] - \frac{\mu}{a(1-e)} $$

To combine these terms, we find a common denominator, which is $2a(1-e)$:

$$ \epsilon = \frac{\mu(1+e) - 2\mu}{2a(1-e)} = \frac{\mu(e-1)}{2a(1-e)} $$

Factoring out a negative sign from the numerator reverses the subtraction, allowing us to perfectly cancel the $(1-e)$ terms:

$$ \epsilon = \frac{-\mu(1-e)}{2a(1-e)} = -\frac{\mu}{2a} $$

This reveals a profound truth: the total specific energy of an elliptical orbit depends solely on the semi-major axis $a$, completely independent of the eccentricity $e$. We substitute this constant energy value back into the original energy equation to finalize the Vis-Viva equation:

$$ \frac{v^2}{2} - \frac{\mu}{r} = -\frac{\mu}{2a} \implies v^2 = \mu \left( \frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a} \right) $$

By taking the square root, we can instantly calculate the velocity $v$ of the spacecraft at any arbitrary distance $r$ without needing to calculate vectors or integrate flight paths. This equation is the primary tool used by NASA and SpaceX to calculate delta-v budgets for interplanetary transfers.

Physical Kinematics & Behaviors

The Vis-Viva equation beautifully models the bounding limits of velocity. If a spacecraft is in a perfectly circular orbit, the radial distance $r$ is always exactly equal to the semi-major axis $a$. Substituting $r = a$ into the equation yields $v^2 = \mu(2/a - 1/a) = \mu/a$, the standard formula for circular velocity. Conversely, if a spacecraft wishes to escape the planet's gravity entirely, the semi-major axis $a$ of its orbit must stretch to infinity. As $a \to \infty$, the term $1/a \to 0$. The equation collapses to $v^2 = 2\mu/r$, defining the exact mathematical boundary for escape velocity.

  • $\epsilon = -\mu / (2a)$
  • $\epsilon = -(3.986 \times 10^{14}) / (2 \times 10^7)$
  • $\epsilon = -3.986 \times 10^{14} / (2 \times 10^7)$
  • $\epsilon = \mathbf{-1.993 \times 10^7 \text{ J/kg}}$

Calculate the velocity of a spacecraft at distance $r = 1 \text{ AU}$ ($1.5 \times 10^{11} \text{ m}$) in an orbit with $a = 2 \text{ AU}$ around the Sun ($\mu_s \approx 1.327 \times 10^{20}$).

  • $v = \sqrt{\mu (2/r - 1/a)}$
  • $v = \sqrt{1.327 \times 10^{20} \times (2/1 - 1/2) \times (1 / 1.5\times10^{11})}$
  • $v \approx \mathbf{42.1 \text{ km/s}}$
